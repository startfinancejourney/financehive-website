Finance Sector

How banks, insurers, and mutual funds actually make money

How They Make Money

The finance sector connects people who have money with people who need it. Banks collect deposits and lend at higher interest rates, keeping the spread as profit. Insurance companies collect premiums from millions, invest that pool, and pay claims from it. Mutual funds pool small investments and charge a management fee on total assets.

Key Players in India

Banks: HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, SBI, Kotak Bank

NBFCs: Bajaj Finance, Muthoot Finance

Insurance: LIC, HDFC Life, ICICI Lombard

AMCs: SBI MF, HDFC AMC, Nippon India AMC

Brokers: Zerodha, Groww, Angel One

3 Terms Every Learner Must Know

NIM (Net Interest Margin): The difference between what a bank earns on loans and what it pays on deposits. Higher NIM = more profitable bank.

NPA (Non-Performing Asset): A loan that the borrower has stopped repaying. High NPAs hurt bank profits badly.

AUM (Assets Under Management): Total money a mutual fund manages. More AUM = more fee income for the fund house.

What to Watch as a Learner

  • NIM and NPA numbers when reading bank results
  • AUM growth for mutual fund companies
  • RBI policy rate changes — they affect all lending rates instantly

Value Chain

The finance sector connects savers with borrowers through trusted intermediaries. In banking, depositors provide funds which banks lend to businesses and individuals at higher rates, keeping the spread as profit. In insurance, millions of policyholders pay small premiums which are pooled and invested — claims are paid from this pool. In mutual funds, small SIP contributions are aggregated into large portfolios managed by professionals. Regulators like RBI, SEBI, and IRDAI oversee every step to ensure savers can trust the system with their money.

Revenue Model

Banks and NBFCs earn through Net Interest Income (NII) — the gap between what they charge on loans and what they pay on deposits. HDFC Bank lends at around 10% and pays 5.5% on deposits — earning 4.5% on every rupee deployed. Insurance companies earn from premiums collected plus investment income earned on that pool before claims are paid. AMCs charge an annual expense ratio of 0.5–2% on total Assets Under Management — as AUM grows, fee income scales automatically with almost no extra cost. Brokerages earn per trade executed, while fintechs earn from merchant fees, loan interest, and distributing financial products through their apps.

Cost Structure

For banks and NBFCs, the biggest cost is the cost of funds — interest paid to depositors and lenders. When RBI raises rates, deposit costs rise immediately, squeezing margins. Provisioning is the most dangerous cost — when loans go bad, banks must set aside money from profits, which can wipe out an entire year's earnings. For insurance companies, claims cost is the largest and most unpredictable expense. For AMCs, costs are mostly fixed — fund managers, analysts, and technology — which means every additional rupee of AUM flows almost entirely to profit. Across all sub-sectors, regulatory compliance costs (CRR, SLR for banks; Solvency Ratio for insurers) are non-negotiable and represent the price of operating in a regulated sector.

Key KPIs

NIM (Net Interest Margin): Net Interest Income divided by average earning assets. The single most watched profitability ratio for banks. Top private banks run at 4–5%. A falling NIM is an early warning sign.

GNPA % (Gross Non-Performing Assets): Bad loans as a percentage of total loans. Above 5% is a warning zone. Above 10% is crisis territory. India's PSU banks hit 15–20% during 2016–2019.

CASA Ratio: Current and Savings Account deposits as a percentage of total deposits. Higher CASA means cheaper funding and stronger margins. HDFC Bank's consistently high CASA is a core competitive advantage.

AUM and SIP Flows: For AMCs, total Assets Under Management drives all fee income. Monthly SIP inflow data shows retail investor health. Rising AUM in all market conditions signals a trusted franchise.

Combined Ratio: For general insurers — claims plus expenses as a percentage of premiums. Below 100% means underwriting profit. Above 100% means the insurer relies on investment income to survive.

ROE (Return on Equity): Net profit divided by equity. Target 15–20% for well-managed banks. A consistently falling ROE signals structural problems.

Growth Drivers

India's credit-to-GDP ratio remains well below developed economies — meaning enormous lending potential is untapped. Retail lending (home loans, personal loans, auto loans) and MSME lending are growing fastest as aspiration-driven Indian consumers buy first homes and vehicles.

India's insurance penetration remains significantly below the global average. Post-COVID awareness, rising healthcare costs, and digital distribution through apps like PolicyBazaar are bringing millions of new households into formal insurance for the first time.

India's mutual fund AUM has grown dramatically driven by the SIP culture and rising financial literacy. Monthly SIP inflows now continue even through market volatility — showing genuine behavioral change among Indian savers.

The JAM Trinity (Jan Dhan, Aadhaar, Mobile) has brought hundreds of millions into formal banking. Converting these account holders into borrowers, investors, and insurance buyers is a decade-long opportunity for every sub-sector of finance.

Risks

NPA Risk (Banks and NBFCs): When borrowers stop repaying, banks must provision from profits. India's PSU banks experienced GNPA ratios of 15–20% during 2016–2019, wiping out years of profits and requiring government recapitalization. This is the most dangerous risk for any lender.

NBFC Liquidity Risk: NBFCs borrow short-term to lend long-term. When funding dries up suddenly — as after IL&FS defaulted in 2018 — NBFCs face an immediate crisis. DHFL collapsed entirely. Sectors they served (real estate, auto, MSME) faced credit crunches for years.

Regulatory Risk: RBI, SEBI, and IRDAI can change rules overnight. Higher risk weights on personal loans (as imposed in late 2023) forced banks to slow growth. SEBI's changes to F&O rules directly hit brokerage volumes. Regulatory risk is the most powerful external force in this sector.

Market Risk (AMCs and Brokerages): When equity markets fall sharply, AMC fee income falls automatically as AUM shrinks. Brokerages see trading volumes collapse in bear markets. Companies with lean cost structures survive — those with high fixed costs face serious stress.

Mis-selling Risk: Agents selling products that maximize commissions rather than customer benefit creates regulatory backlash and reputational damage. IRDAI and SEBI have both imposed commission caps to address this.
