IT Services Sector

Selling Indian brainpower to the world — one line of code at a time

How They Make Money

Indian IT companies earn by doing technology work for global companies at a fraction of what it would cost those companies to hire locally. A US bank that would pay $250,000 per year for a New York engineer pays roughly $15,000-25,000 for an equally skilled engineer in Pune — delivered through TCS, Infosys, or Wipro. Revenue flows in three ways. Time and Material contracts pay for every hour an engineer works — flexible and dominant for large ongoing projects. Fixed Price contracts deliver a defined outcome for a fixed fee — more risk for the IT company. Managed Services contracts pay a fixed monthly retainer to run a client's entire IT systems for 3-7 years — the most valuable because they are sticky, recurring, and hard to replace. The real compounding happens through account mining — once TCS wins a banking client for one service, they sell cloud, cybersecurity, analytics, and AI services to the same client over years, expanding revenue without winning new customers.

Key Players in India

Large Cap IT: TCS (Tata Consultancy Services), Infosys, HCL Technologies, Wipro, Tech Mahindra

Mid Cap IT: LTIMindtree, Mphasis, Coforge, Persistent Systems

Engineering and R&D Services: LTTS (L&T Technology Services), Tata Elxsi, Cyient, KPIT Technologies

Key Clients (global): Banks, airlines, retailers, healthcare companies, telecom operators — mostly in the US (50-60% of revenues) and Europe

3 Terms Every Learner Must Know

EBIT Margin: Earnings Before Interest and Tax as a percentage of revenue — the operating profitability of the business. TCS and Infosys maintain 20-26% EBIT margins consistently. A falling EBIT margin over multiple quarters signals rising employee costs or pricing pressure from clients.

Attrition Rate: The percentage of employees who left voluntarily in a year. A healthy rate is 10-15%. Above 20% means engineers are being pulled away by competitors — training costs spike, project quality suffers, and margins compress. The 2021-22 attrition crisis across Indian IT showed how destructive high attrition can be.

TCV (Total Contract Value): The total value of new contracts signed in a quarter. This is the forward-looking engine of future revenue. Strong TCV means revenue growth will continue. Declining TCV is an early warning that the growth engine is slowing — analysts watch this even more closely than current revenue.

What to Watch as a Learner

  • US economic health — roughly 50-60% of Indian IT revenue comes from the US, so any US slowdown directly hits deal wins and revenue growth
  • Attrition and utilization numbers in quarterly results — high attrition and low utilization are the two biggest margin destroyers in this sector
  • TCV of new deal wins — tells you whether future revenue is being built or shrinking
  • AI strategy of each company — how each major IT company is positioning for AI is the most important long-term question in the sector today

Value Chain

The IT services journey starts when a global company identifies a technology need — rebuilding their banking app, migrating to cloud, or complying with new data regulations. Indian IT companies have large sales teams based near clients in New York and London who identify these needs and respond to formal Requests for Proposals. Winning large deals often takes months. Once the contract is signed, a team of Indian engineers is assembled in Chennai, Pune, or Bengaluru — predominantly offshore for cost efficiency, with a small onsite team at the client location for relationship management. Engineers build, test, and deliver the software or system. After delivery, the real long-term money begins — maintenance and support contracts that can run 10-15 years, providing predictable recurring revenue that looks like a financial annuity. The highest value is created at the consulting and strategy stage — a consultant billing $250 per hour earns far more per person than a maintenance engineer billing $40 per hour. The smartest IT companies win a consulting engagement and then convert it into a large multi-year execution contract — the full-stack model TCS and Infosys are building.

Revenue Model

Three billing models drive all IT revenue. Time and Material is the dominant model — clients pay per hour per engineer deployed. If TCS deploys 500 engineers at $50 per hour, revenue is predictable and scales with team size. Fixed Price contracts deliver a defined outcome for a fixed fee — if delivery takes longer than estimated, profit shrinks. Managed Services pay a fixed monthly retainer — Infosys managing a European airline's entire IT for €5 million per month creates annuity-like recurring income. The revenue base is deeply sticky because long-term maintenance contracts keep billing even when new project wins slow. Cross-selling amplifies growth — once inside a Fortune 500 client, the IT company sells application development, then cloud migration, then cybersecurity, then AI tools — mining the same relationship for decades of expanding revenue without the cost of winning a new client.

Cost Structure

Employee salaries are the dominant cost at 55-65% of total revenue — making IT fundamentally a people business where the primary asset walks out the door every evening. Engineers expect 5-8.5% salary hikes annually. Hiring and training is significant — large IT companies hire 70,000-150,000 fresh engineering graduates every year, each requiring months of training before they become billable. Bench cost — paying salaries to engineers not yet deployed on client projects — is a real drag on margins during slowdowns. Infrastructure (massive campuses, high-speed internet, software licenses, collaboration tools) is the second major cost. Sales and client acquisition in the US and Europe is expensive — deal pursuit can take 12-18 months and may not result in a win. Currency is a two-edged sword — a weaker rupee automatically boosts margins since revenues are in dollars but costs are in rupees, while a stronger rupee compresses them.

Key KPIs

Revenue Growth %: How fast total revenue is growing year on year. Constant currency growth (stripping out exchange rate effects) is the purest measure. Strong large-cap IT companies grow 10-15% annually in good times and 4-7% during slowdowns.

EBIT Margin %: Operating profitability. TCS and Infosys maintain 20-26%. A falling margin over multiple quarters signals rising attrition costs or client pricing pressure.

Attrition Rate %: Employees leaving voluntarily per year. Healthy is 10-15%. Above 20% signals a talent war destroying margins — as happened industry-wide in 2021-22.

Utilization Rate %: Percentage of billable employees actually working on client projects versus sitting on the bench. Target is 80-85%. A falling utilization rate directly crushes margins — idle engineers are pure cost.

TCV of New Deals: Total Contract Value of new contracts signed. The single best forward-looking revenue indicator. A strong TCV pipeline means growth continues. A declining TCV is an early warning before revenue growth slows.

Revenue per Employee: Total revenue divided by headcount. Rising revenue per employee means the company is moving toward higher-value services or using AI to improve efficiency — increasingly the most important long-term metric in the sector.

Growth Drivers

Global digital transformation is the single most powerful structural driver. Banks are replacing 30-year-old core systems. Retailers are rebuilding supply chains using AI. Hospitals are adopting electronic health records. Each transformation requires years of IT work — and Indian companies are best positioned to execute them at scale.

Cloud computing is the biggest platform shift in a generation. Almost every large company is migrating from on-premise servers to AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud. Cloud migration requires massive IT services work and is currently one of the fastest-growing revenue categories for all major Indian IT companies.

India's cost advantage remains structural and powerful. A San Francisco engineer earns $150,000-$250,000 per year. An equally skilled Pune engineer earns roughly $12,000-$24,000. Even accounting for management overhead and profit, Indian IT delivery costs 40-60% less than local hiring in the US.

AI is creating entirely new categories of IT services — building AI models, deploying AI agents in enterprise settings, managing AI infrastructure, and integrating AI into existing workflows. Every major Indian IT company has launched large AI practices (Infosys Topaz, TCS AI Cloud, WiproAI360) to capture this demand.

New geographies are diversifying revenues — Saudi Arabia and UAE's massive digitalization programs, Japan's growing outsourcing market, and Southeast Asia are reducing dependence on the US economic cycle.

Risks

Global Recession and IT Budget Cuts: When the US economy slows, IT spending is among the first discretionary budgets to be cut. New project starts are frozen, existing vendors face price renegotiation, and deal pipelines take longer to close. The 2022-23 period showed exactly this — US interest rate hikes cooled corporate IT budgets and Indian IT revenue growth decelerated sharply.

Attrition: High employee turnover — above 20% annually — forces companies to continuously replace experienced engineers with freshers. Training costs spike, project delivery quality suffers, and client relationships are disrupted. This was the defining operational crisis of 2021-22 across the entire Indian IT sector.

AI Automation Risk: AI tools can increasingly write code, test software, and handle customer queries — tasks that currently require human engineers. If AI reduces billable engineer-hours needed per project, the core unit of IT revenue could shrink. This is the most profound structural risk the sector faces over the next decade.

Pricing Pressure: Clients use competitive bidding to squeeze billing rates. Newer competitors from Eastern Europe, the Philippines, and Latin America also compete on cost. Legacy service lines like application maintenance face persistent billing rate stagnation.

Currency Fluctuation: A stronger rupee means the same dollar revenue converts to fewer rupees — reported margins appear lower. A weaker rupee is a tailwind. Companies hedge partially but currency movement remains a meaningful driver of quarterly profitability.

Visa Restrictions: H-1B visa limits restrict how many Indian engineers can be deployed onsite at US client locations. Policy shifts force companies to hire locally at much higher costs, directly compressing margins.
